AIR POLLUTION AND INNOVATION

Fig 1: Annual average number of patent filings per NUTS-3 region

Fig 2: Annual average fine particulate matter per NUTS-3 region

Together with my co-author Dennis Verhoeven, we estimate the impact of air pollution on the aggregated inventive output in Europe. Existing estimates of the economic costs of air pollution do not account for its effect on inventive output. However, estimating the causal impact of air pollution on innovation is complex due to many unobserved factors that are correlated with region’s economy and pollution. For instance, regions with a high economic activity (including innovation) produce high levels of air pollution. Thus, simply regressing regions air pollution levels on its inventive outcomes would lead to an underestimation of the negative effects of air pollution on innovation. To mitigate such concerns, we estimate an instrumental variable regression exploiting two weather phenomena that induce random variation in air pollution. Our estimation reveals that a decrease in exposure to small particulate matter of 0.17 µg/m3 -- the average yearly reduction in Europe -- leads to 1.7% more patented inventions. This increase in the inventive output corresponds to a value of 21.3 billion euro.

SIGNALING VALUE OF LEGAL FORM IN DEBT FINANCING

Deregulation of capital requirements does not only lead to an increase in entrepreneurship, it also entails a substantial substitution effect where entrepreneurs are more likely to opt for a legal form with low instead of high paid-in capital. In this paper, we show that firms that choose for a limited liability company with low paid-in capital experience a lower access to debt financing compared to their high paid-in capital counterparts. These findings are robust to using different estimation techniques, including a difference-in-difference and regression discontinuity design. Our findings are consistent with the idea that credit providers use the entrepreneur’s legal form choice as a screening device to sort out perceived high-risk firms from low-risk firms. Our findings contribute to a more nuanced understanding of the impact of deregulation of capital requirements on newly established firms.
